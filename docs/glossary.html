Glossary

Term Definition
2:1 clay minerals type of a phyllosilicate mineral with the structure of 1 octahedral layer in between 2 tetrahedral layers.
Aggregate physical body that is clearly distinguishable from its surroundings.
Alluvium/alluvial Soil-forming deposits transported and laid down by a flowing watercourse
Apedal Soil material that does not form peds. Natural separation of soil aggregates occurs either at very small (<6 mm) or large dimensions (>100 mm), or the soil structure unit is not formed by pedogenic processes. In the case of sand, a soil structure unit can also equate to an individual grain.
Base saturation proportion of the CEC that is occupied by Ca, Mg, K, and Na
CEC abbreviation for cation exchange capacity; cation exchange is the interchange between a cation in solution and another cation held by electrostatic forces on the surface of any material but particularly clay minerals or organic matter. Expressed as centimoles of charge per kilogram of soil.
Calcareous refers to the presence of CaCO3 minerals.
Chroma Within the Munsell Color system, chroma describes the degree of colour saturation.
Dry bulk density Density of the soil material at a defined dry state. This is commonly expressed in units of g cm-3 or Mg m-3
ECEC Effective cation exchange capacity; equal to the sum of cation-exchangeable Ca, Mg, K, Na, and Al at ambient pH.
Failure A measure of soil consistence. Failure is the manner in which unconfined soil yields to increasing stress.
Fluidity A measure of soil consistence. Fluidity is the tendency of soil to behave as a liquid under high soil moisture conditions.
HCl Hydrogen chloride; an acid frequently used in soil analysis to detect the presence of calcium carbonate (CaCO3) due to the reaction releasing CO2.
Holocene current geological epoch that started ~12,000 years ago. On average, global temperatures have been higher than in the Pleistocene.
Hue Within the Munsell Color system, hue describes how red, yellow, green or blue soil material is.
KCl-extractable/exchangeable aluminium Aluminium that can be made soluble by Sodium Chloride as solvent. It approximates the fraction of aluminium in the soil that is readily accessible to biota. In acidic soils this can reach levels that are toxic to biota.
Mafic NA
P retention measure of the ability of the soil to rapidly remove phosphorus from the soil solution. Soils with high retention values usually have high amounts of short-range-order minerals. Beyond the rapid removal, these soils are also susceptible to retain large proportions of soil phosphorus in a form that is less accessible to plants.
Packing of particles A measure of soil consistence. It indicates how tightly soil particles (or peds) occupy a space. Closely packed units leave smaller spaces between them than loosely packed ones, leading to decreased porosities and greater bulk densities.
Ped/pedal/pedality Refers to soil aggregates as a single soil structural unit that can be observed by the human eye, is described in geometric terms (size, shape, arrangement), and the result of pedogenic processes. The cohesive forces holding individual units together is greater than the adhesive forces among them. Examples are prismatic, blocky, polyhedral, speroidal.
Penetration resistance A measure of soil consistence. Penetration resistance is the capacity of the soil, in its natural confined state, to resist penetration by rigid objects.
Plasticity A measure of soil consistence. Plasticity is the capacity of soil to change shape (or deform) continuously when stress is applied and to retain that shape after the removal of stress.
Pleistocene The previous geological epoch that started 2.6 million years ago and ended ~12,000 years ago.
Pore/porosity Volume of soil not occupied by solid particles. Pores are of varying shapes and sizes, and are either occupied by liquids or gases.
Quartzo-feldspathic refers to rocks that are dominated by quartz and feldspar minerals
Reactive Aluminium Test Field test to determine the relative amounts of short-range-order minerals in the soil
Regolith Any loose, unconsolidated material at the earth’s surface above solid bedrock. This comprises saprolite but also material that has been transported (e.g., colluvial material)
Saprolite Bedrock that has been weathering in its present position (i.e., autochtonous). It has lost its original rock strength and coherence but retains the rock structure.
Sensitivity A measure of soil consistence. The sensitivity of soil material is the ratio of its natural strength to its strength when remoulded at the same water content.
Slaking The process of disaggregation/collapse of a soil specimen after submerging it in water.
Soil series Grouping of soil types with similar model profiles, similar temperature and moisture regimes, and the same or very similar parent material
Soil strength A measure of soil consistence. The strength of soil material can be estimated by the resistance-to-crushing test.
Sphagnum species Species of the peat-forming plant genus Sphagnum
Stickiness A measure of soil consistence. Stickiness is the degree to which remoulded soil adheres to foreign objects.
Value Within the Munsell Color system, value describes the ligthness/darkness of a soil colour.
aeolian Soil-forming deposits transported and laid down by atmospheric currents (i.e., wind) such as loess, dune sand, and most tephra
aeration supply of air to the soil
allochthonous material Material that originated at a distance from its present location
aluminium-humus complexes mobile compunds consisting of aluminium and organic matter that are from in soils and are common soil constituents in NZ soils.
andesite/andesitic refers to an intermediate, igneous rock that occurs as solid bedrock or pyroclastic material (e.g., as ash) in NZ.
argillite Sedimentary rock derived from clay- and silt-sized particles
artefact Items manufactured for use, or part of the process of manufacture
ash see pyroclastic material
autochthonous Formed in its present position
basalt/basaltic mafic, igneous volcanic rock
bases in the NZSC refering to the elements Ca, Mg, K, and Na.
bifurcated Split into two
blocks see pyroclastic material
boundary Refers to soil horizons and describes the transitions between them; shape and distinctness of a boundary are categorised when describing soils.
cementation A soil process physically binding together soil particles (or peds) by the means of a chemical binding agent. Common binding agents that form in NZ soils are calcium carbonate, silica, and metal oxides.
cinders see pyroclastic material
clay illuviation Enriched in allochtonous clay (e.g., argillic horizon, cutanoxidic horizon)
coating a thin layer on peds faces, surfaces of individual grains or lining pore surfaces that are enriched in constituents that result from pedogenic processes (e.g., clay coatings, humus coatings)
colluvial refers to an unconsolidated sediment from material that has been transported due to gravity on sloping ground. It usually has a heterogenous appearance (e.g., mix of coarse and fine-earth grain sizes).
concretions cemented, concentric bodies within the soil that are enriched in constituents that result from pedogenic processes (e.g., secondary oxides, calcium carbonate)
cross-stratification Sediment layers that are inclined in the direction of water or wind transport. They cut across the main, usually more horizontal bedding plane.
crystalline Arrangement of atoms in a three-dimensional and repeating pattern.
current ripples Wave-like sedimentary structures with crests and throughs aligned perpendicular to a current of water or wind.
decalcification removal of CaCO3 from the soil, most commonly by soil water
dispersion The tendency of soil particles to separate into colloidal (< 1 µm) size materials when mechanically agitated in suspension. Soils which are highly dispersive show a tendency to crust and clog finer pores impeding drainag. This can be tested in the field.
drainage Drainage indicates the frequency and duration of soil wetness under natural conditions, at different depths in the soil profile, throughout the year, or seasonally. The relative classification is based on the soil redox state as indicated by the soil colour, presence of mottles, and organic matter.
electrical conductivity The ability of the soil to conduct an electric current. In soils this is commonly measured in units of mS cm-1.
feldspar Silicate mineral widely occuring in NZ rocks.
felsic NA
field capacity The soil moisture content after the drainage from a saturated state (i.e., all pores filled with water) due to gravity is reduced to a rate ≤1 mm day-1.
fine-earth Individual soil particles with a size of <2 mm, grouped into sand, silt, and clay.
footslope concave, increasingly flatter part of a slope at its base.
foreset beds sediment deposited by a flowing water at the end of the stream channel when forming a delta
fragmental Substrate consisting of >35% (by volume) of coarse fragments (>2 mm)
goethite a secondary iron oxide with a crystalline structure. One of the most common iron oxides in soils.
granite felsic, igneous rock with prominent mineral crystals
horizon Soil horizons structure a soil profile into predominantly horizontal sections. Within-horizon variability of soil properties (e.g., texture, consitence, colour) is minimised while between-horizon differences are maximised. Individual horizons or the combination of different horizons reflect the pedogenic processes operating in the soil.
hump-hollowing practice of recontouring a landsurface to improve hydraulic conditions of a soil (e.g., increase permeability/conductivity of the soil, removal of excess surface water)
humus other term commonly used for soil organic matter
hydraulic conductivity See also apermeability class. It refers to the velocity that water can move through the soil. Hydraulic conductivity is commonly reported at defined moisture contents or tensions (e.g., saturated, unsaturated).
hydrothermal activity Release of heated liquids and gases to the surface as a result of volcanic and tectonic activity
igneous Rocks from solidified magma or lava.
ignimbrite Volcanic deposit formed by pyroclastic flow.
illite A 2:1, non-expandable clay mineral with a comparably low CEC
in-situ formed in place, similar use as autochthonous
induration A measure of soil consistence. Induration is the degree to which soil particles are held together by some combination of cementation and close packing.
interlayered/interstratified minerals refers to variants of clays minerals deviating from their standard form by incorporating additional layers into their structure (e.g., aluminium-interlayered vermiculite). This sustains structural integrity as the chemical conditons in the soil change over time as part of soil formation.
jarosite iron-containing sulphate mineral that forms in soils exposed to acidic and sulfur-rich waters, usually linked to past or present coastal environments.
kaolin-group group of clay minerals consisting of only octahedral sheets (i.e., 1:1 clay minerals) and have a low CEC
lamella thin layer (≥5 mm) of clay-enriched soil material that commonly occurs as part of multiple lamellae and can be a type of argillic horizon.
lamination bedding plane(s) of sediment deposited by a current of water or wind
lapilli see pyroclastic material
linear expandibility measure describing the ability of soil material to shrink and swell under different moisture states
liquid limit Soil moisture content at which the soil starts to behave as a liquid
lithification/lithified refers to the process in which sediments compact under pressure, and gradually become solid sedimentary rock. Liquids that are trapped in pores are gradually expelled.
lithological discontinuity Change of parent material within a soil. Such changes can be differences in the composition or deposition of a sediment (age, process of deposition). Such discintinuities are frequently indicated by contrasting grain sizes, colours and mineralogy.
litter above-ground plant detritus, mainly leaves and stem material.
loess wind-transported sediment dominated by silt-sized particles
low chroma colours refers to the Munsell Color system; defined as a colour with a moist chroma of ≤2, or moist chroma 3 with value ≥6.
low-activity clay Clay minerals, like kaolinite, with a CEC typically below that of 2:1 clay minerals.
macroporosity Soil pores of >60 µm diameter that hold water at very low tensions and thus drain rapidly.
matric potential This is equal to the potential energy due to the attraction of water to the solid matrix through absorption and capillary forces.
matrix The solid soil components in their entirety, but often specified further (e.g., fine-earth matrix) to recognise the more homogenous components of a soil (i.e., the groundmass), in which soil material with different properties (e.g., coarser graine sizes, different colour) can be embedded in.
mica the primary mineral equivalent to illite.
midden Deposit of a concentration of discared or buried food remains (e.g., shellfish, animal bones), ash/charcoal from fires, and/or broken or worn-out tools
mineral soil Soil material that has ≤18% of organic carbon in the fine-earth
mineralogy class classification of minerals that form in NZ soils.
mor Organic surface layer above the mineral soil consisting of plant matter at various, but predominantly early stages of decomposition; biological activity and fertility is low and the layer is strongly acidic.
mottles soil material embedded in the matrix and recognised by the contrast in colour
nodule Similar to a concretion but lacks the concentric internal structure.
oxidation part of a redox reaction; describes the loss of electron(s) by an element. The element is being oxidised.
papa kāinga Te reo: home, village
parent material The natural or anthropogenic material from which the soil forms.
particle size A measure of the sizes of individual soil particles for both the fine-earth and particles of >2 mm. The main classes of the fine earth are sand, silt and clay.
peaty refers to soil or soil material containing a significant amount of organic material that accumualted under wet conditions. Aside form being used in such a broad context, quantity and type of peat is defined in the NZSC (e.g., peaty textures, peaty topsoil).
pedogenic refers to the combined soil formation processes and specific components that form as the result of these processes (e.g., secondary oxides, clay or carbonate minerals)
pedon A pedon has the smallest volume for which one should describe and sample the soil to represent the nature and arrangement of its horizons and variability in the properties.
permeability class The permeability refers to the hydraulic conductivity of the soil water. The velocity that water can move through the soil is affected by multiple factors, such as moisture content, soil structure, porosity, soil consistence, and particle size distribution. Common classes of permeability are slow (≤4 mm h-1), moderate (>4-18 and >18-72 mm h-1) and rapid (>72 mm h-1).
phonolite felsic igneous rocks dominated by felspars and lacking quartz
phosphate fixation Long-term immobilisation of phosphate in the soil by removing it from the soil water and incorporating it into secondary soil minerals. This make it less accessible for plants and microbes.
phyllosilicate Silicate minerals that have a sheet-like structure, a typical appearance of clay minerals in soils.
plaggen human-modified soils where grass sods (combined with manure and other organic material) have been mixed in with mineral substrate; typically manifests itself in an organic matter-rich, dark surface layer.
plant-available-water capacity The proportion of the soil volume drained between the pressure levels of -10 and -1500 kPa (i.e., the water held at tensions between 10 and 1500 kPa) but limited by the soil volume of the rooting depth. If not explicitly specified, the rooting depth for agricultural landuse is assumed to be 60 cm.
plastic limit The maximum moisture content at which a soil shows cracks when remolded. Above this limit, soil shows plastic deformation.
primary when refering to minerals (e.g., primary carbonates), indicates minerals that did not form in the soil but are directly derived from the soil parent material.
pugging Plastic deformation of soil, commonly as a consequence of stock treading or use of heavy machinery when the soil has a high moisture content. Also see plasticity.
pumice see pyroclastic material
pyroclastic material Material ejected from volcanoes during explosive eruptions. Due to rapid cooling, minerals cannot form crystalline structures. These materials can be classified according to size (small to large: ash, lapilli, block, bomb) or other properties (e.g., material with high porosity and different composition and sizes: scoria, tuff, pumice, cinders; material with low porosity and different composition: obsidian, volcanic glass, rhyolite; origin of material: air-fall deposits like pumice or ash and pyroclastic flow deposits like ignimbrite). See also tephric material as a diagnostic criteria.
pā Te reo: fortified settlement
readily available water capacity The proportion of the total soil volume drained between the pressure levels of -10 and -100 kPa (i.e., the water held at tensions between 10 and 100 kPa). This is the proportion of water that is held at moderate tensions, and thus most easily accessible to plants.
redox short for 'reduction-oxidation'; describes chemical reactions involving the transfer of electrons between elements that can occur at various redox states (e.g., iron, carbon, nitrogen)
reduction part of a redox reaction; describes the gain of electron(s) by an element. The element is being reduced.
rhizomorph Hyphae of fungi that resemble plant roots.
rhyolite/rhyolitic see ignimbrite and pyroclastic material
root class The size and abundance of roots in the soil.
salinisation Process of increasing salt levels in the soil, mainly as result of evaporation of soil moisture. It can reach concentrations that are toxic for most biota except specialist species.
schist a metamorphic rock with a sheet-like appearance formed under pressure and heat
scoria see pyroclastic material
scoured surface surfaces being eroded by flowing water
secondary minerals minerals that have formed in the soil as part of the soil formation process, these are mainly clay minerals, oxides, and salts.
secondary oxides in the NZSC refers to metal oxides (i.e., a chemical compound containing a metal and oxygen) that have formed in the soil as part of the soil formation process (e.g., iron oxides).
sedimentary rocks Rocks that are formed from the lithification of unconsolidated mineral or organic or biogenic deposits
seepage below-ground current of water reaching the land surface, occurs commonly due to increases in groundwater level or at the base of slopes.
short-range-order/amorphous/poorly crystalline/non-crystalline minerals/allophane-imogolite-ferrihydrite type of clay minerals that do not have a crystalline structure like most minerals, or only at a very small scale. The surface areas of these minerals is very high, making them chemically very reactive.
silcate mineral Minerals containing silica (Si).
skeletal Substrate consisting of >35% (by volume) of coarse fragments (>2 mm)
skeletans Sand or silt grains concentrated at the surfaces of peds, voids or cracks; they may be residual concentrations resulting from removal of finer particles, or sand and/or silt may have moved in from horizons above.
smectite A group of 2:1 clay minerals with a lower CEC than vermiculite.
soil structure Structure describes the spatial organisation of soil material (i.e., pedal and apedal material), including the voids between the solids.
solum The soil material that is modified by current pedogenic procesess; it excludes the C horizon.
specific surface area Total surface area per unit mass or volume
sum of bases The sum of cation-exchangeable basic cations (Ca, Mg, K, Na)
tension In the context of soil moisture describes the negative of the pressure that has to be overcome to separate water from surfaces of soil particles. In soils it is normally measured in units of kPa.
texture Refers to the proportions of different particle sizes in the fine-earth fraction of the soil. It is an approximation usually assessed by hand and expressed as a texture class in a ternary system (i.e., as proportions of sand, silt, clay). Examples are silt loam, loamy clay or clay loam.
total available water capacity The proportion of the total soil volume drained between the pressure levels of -10 and -1500 kPa (i.e., the water held at tensions between 10 and 1500 kPa)
total carbon The total mount of carbon in the soil. This comprises carbon in organic (e.g., plant residues) and inorganic (e.g., carbonate salts) form.
tuffaceous containing volcanic ash
ultramafic NA
variable-charge refers to surfaces of minerals or organic matter whose surface charge can vary depending on soil pH. At low pH, the surface charge is positive, at high pH the surfaces become negatively charged.
vascular plant Plants with highly differentiated conductive cells that operate as tubes or vessels (xylem, phloem) transporting water and nutrients throughout the plant body.
vermiculite A 2:1 clay mineral that is expandable (i.e., it shrinks and swells) and has a comparably high CEC.
volcanic bombs see pyroclastic material
volcanic glass see pyroclastic material
weathering rind physical and chemical alteration of the outer section of rock, appearing as a rim parallel to the outer surface. Usually identified by a colour change due to oxidation of metals.
welded layer refers to pumice or ignimbrite deposits that due to heat and pressure have reduced porosity and become lithified.
wetland These are areas of poor drainage or where water accumulates. Ecologically, these are permanently or intermittently wet areas, shallow water, and land water margins that support a natural ecosystem of plants and animals that are adapted to wet conditions.
